# Westmag Brand Brief

*Anchor doc for all visual decisions — variants, launch materials, social,
press. Last updated 2026-05-12 from Matt.*

## Aesthetic
- **Clean and restrained.** No complex interactions, no motion graphics.
  Subtle fade-ins are OK; scroll-jacking, parallax, hover spectaculars
  are not.
- **Distinct, confident aesthetic.** The brand has its own thing going.
  See westmag.com for the existing logo and brand identity. A formal
  brand guide exists (assets to be added when shared).

## Tone
- **American Dynamism** — confident, serious, forward-leaning.
- **Both defense AND commercial.** Drones and robotics. Tone must not
  alienate either side. **No clichéd militarism.** No camo, no
  tactical-bro flourishes, no "war-fighter" copy, no firearms imagery.
- **Bold, functional, proud.**

## Era reference
- **50s–60s American industrial.** Hoover-era manufacturing prestige.
  IBM under Paul Rand. Bethlehem Steel. GE / Westinghouse / Boeing of
  that period. Standard Oil. The poster art of *Fortune* magazine.
- **Specific inspiration: SeaLand Industries** — Malcolm McLean's
  shipping-container company. Nation-building confidence,
  industrial-ingenuity ethos, trade-catalog functional design.

## Translates to (design tokens)
- **Typography:** bold geometric sans-serif headlines (Inter outlined
  uppercase reads correctly for this era). IBM Plex Mono for body /
  technical specs reinforces the trade-catalog feel.
- **Color:** Restrained palette. The orange `#ff3905` is the *one* hot
  accent — use sparingly, like a Bethlehem Steel red. Pair with cream,
  charcoal, ink-black. Avoid navy → it's the cliché militarism color.
  Avoid camo greens.
- **Layout:** Functional grids. Poster-style blocks. Negative space as
  a confidence move, not as a stylistic flourish.
- **Decoration:** Almost none. No chevrons, no SVG flourishes, no
  geometric ornament. The typography and the grid do the work.
- **Imagery:** Working factories. Motor cross-sections. Black-and-white
  or muted-color industrial photography. NOT renders of soldiers, drones
  in combat, or anything that screams "defense first."

## Anti-patterns (what to NOT do)
- ❌ Complex hover interactions
- ❌ Motion graphics (animated SVG illustrations, looping videos beyond
  the hero, parallax scroll)
- ❌ Camo, tactical, war-fighter, military jargon
- ❌ Soft pastel gradients, SaaS aesthetics, Stripe-style polish
- ❌ Heavy chrome / gradient bevels / glassmorphism
- ❌ Robot mascots / playful characters (the Daft-Punk avatar idea was
  killed)

## What the brand is *for*
- Drone OEMs (commercial AND defense)
- Robotics companies (industrial, humanoid)
- Defense primes (program offices, USG buyers)
- Top engineering talent (American Dynamism-leaning candidates)
- a16z, Founders Fund, and the wider investor community in this thesis
